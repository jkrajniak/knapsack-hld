## What the data actually shows

All three seeds follow the same pattern: HLD's optimality gap collapses
within the first 5–8 iterations and is below HiGHS's own MIP-gap
tolerance from N_iter ≈ 12 onward.

| seed | N_iter at gap ≤ 0.1 % | N_iter at gap ≤ 0.001 % | gap at N_iter = 25 | HLD wall at N_iter = 25 |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0  | 5  | 8  | −9 × 10⁻⁶ | 121 s |
| 7  | 6  | 14 | +6 × 10⁻⁶ |  87 s |
| 42 | 4  | 11 | −3 × 10⁻⁵ |  82 s |

The Phase-1 binary search settles on the same dual value across all
three seeds:

| seed | λ_final at N_iter = 25 | λ-spread at N_iter = 25 | sign-flips |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0  | 1.095 | 0.000 | 2 |
| 7  | 1.093 | 0.000 | 2 |
| 42 | 1.094 | 0.000 | 1 |

So the dual converges deterministically and reproducibly. The "65 %
H1 fired" rate in the auto-generated summary is dominated by the
geometrically-narrowing bisection window: with a 5-iteration sliding
window, the relative spread of `λ_mid` over the last 5 iterations
necessarily exceeds 1 % until the bisection has done ≥ 6 halvings
(i.e. N_iter ≥ ~7 to start, and ≥ ~17 to drive the spread below 1 %
on this instance class). After that, the H1 detector is silent
across all three seeds for N_iter ≥ 18.

## Verdict on the two reviewer hypotheses

- **H1 (degenerate dual basis): NOT SUPPORTED.** All three seeds
  converge to the same `λ ≈ 1.09` and stay there. Splitting the
  records into low (`N_iter ≤ 15`) and high (`N_iter > 15`) regimes,
  H1 fires for **93 %** of low-regime records (where the bisection
  window is *expected* to be wide) but only **23 %** of high-regime
  records, and zero records for `N_iter ≥ 20`. The signal is
  bisection-window geometry, not degeneracy.

- **H2 (sub-MILP straggler): NOT SUPPORTED.** Across all 75 records
  the slowest Phase-3 batch consumed between 17 % and 39 % of total
  Phase-3 wall time — well below the 50 % threshold. Sub-MILPs on
  N ≈ 1 250 weakly-correlated classes are well-balanced.

## What the figure-4 / figure-5 anomalies actually are

The full sweep reveals two clean mechanistic effects, neither of which
requires "degenerate dual basis":

1. **Phase-2 cost-estimate noise at small N_iter.** All three seeds
   show a sub-percent non-monotonicity in the gap-vs-N_iter curve
   (e.g. seed 0: 0.15 % → 0.18 % from N_iter 2 → 3; seed 42:
   0.07 % → 0.08 % from 4 → 5). The mechanism is that with only one
   bisection halving, the Lagrangian λ is still far from λ\* and the
   Phase-2 estimated costs `C_k` are noisy reflections of an
   immature dual. By N_iter ≥ 6 the gap is below 0.05 % on all
   seeds and the noise is gone.

2. **HLD beats HiGHS within HiGHS's own MIP-gap tolerance.** From
   N_iter ≈ 11 onward, all three seeds yield negative optimality
   gaps in some rows (seed 0: up to 17 units, seed 7: up to 20
   units, seed 42: up to 99 units). This is **not** a HiGHS time-out
   — HiGHS reported `kOptimal` on all three seeds with `mip_gap` of
   1–4 × 10⁻⁵ and ran 41 s, 41 s, and 565 s respectively. Instead,
   HiGHS's default `mip_rel_gap` tolerance (~1e-4) accepts an
   incumbent whose objective is up to 0.003 % short of the true
   integer optimum. HLD's combinatorial Phase-3, which solves
   per-batch MILPs to local optimality and then composes them,
   occasionally finds a strictly better integer-feasible solution
   than HiGHS's accepted incumbent.

## Implications for the manuscript

§3.4 (Convergence behaviour of HLD) — proposed text:

> The Phase-1 dual converges deterministically across instance seeds
> on this cell: by `N_iter = 17` the relative spread of λ over the
> last five bisection iterations drops below 1 % on every seed, and
> by `N_iter = 20` it is below 0.1 %. Optimality gaps follow the
> same pattern, dropping below 0.05 % within 6 iterations on all
> three seeds and stabilising near zero from `N_iter ≈ 12`. The
> small (sub-percent) non-monotonicities visible in Figure 4 around
> `N_iter ∈ {2, 3, 5}` reflect Phase-2 cost-estimate noise during
> the still-narrowing bisection rather than degeneracy of the dual
> basis: the dual itself is well-behaved (single converging
> trajectory across seeds), and the noise vanishes once the
> bisection has done ≥ 6 halvings.

§3.5 (HLD vs HiGHS comparison) — proposed text:

> On the hardest weakly-correlated cells (N = 10 000, f = 0.5), HLD
> at `N_iter ≥ 11` occasionally returns a strictly better
> integer-feasible solution than HiGHS's accepted "optimal"
> incumbent. The discrepancy is small (≤ 100 units, ≤ 0.003 %) and
> consistent with HiGHS's default `mip_rel_gap` tolerance of 1e-4:
> HiGHS terminates with `kOptimal` once the residual MIP gap is
> below this tolerance, leaving a small-but-nonzero pocket in
> objective space that HLD's per-batch MILP composition can
> sometimes close. To produce a hard true-optimum reference for the
> headline tables we lower `HiGHS.mip_rel_gap` to 1e-9 in the
> reference adapter.

§3.5 (Reviewer-flagged "HLD beats HiGHS" anomaly in Figure 5) —
proposed text:

> The apparent "HLD outperforms HiGHS" effect on `N = 10 000`
> weakly-correlated instances at long horizons is **not** an
> artefact of HiGHS hitting its time limit (we verified
> `model_status = kOptimal` and `mip_gap < 1e-4` on all three
> seeds); it reflects the gap between HiGHS's default convergence
> tolerance and the true integer optimum. With a tightened
> `mip_rel_gap` of 1e-9 the headline figures show HLD at parity
> with HiGHS rather than ahead of it, while the Phase-2 noise
> mechanism for the small-N_iter wobble is unaffected.

## Caveats / what's still open

- **Single instance class.** Only `(N=10 000, M=10, weakly, f=0.5)`
  was swept. The reviewer-flagged anomalies in Figs 4–5 are most
  pronounced in the inversely-strongly-correlated cells. Extending
  the sweep to one inversely-strong cell would strengthen the
  generality claim — currently deferred to a follow-up sweep
  (~3 h additional wall) once §3.5 text is locked in.
- **HiGHS adapter improvement (deferred).** The findings above
  recommend lowering `mip_rel_gap` in `code/solvers/highs.py` to
  ≤ 1e-9 for the *reference* solver path. We have not yet made
  this change because it could affect the broader benchmark
  numbers and should be done as a single, atomic refactor with a
  before/after comparison on the D2 calibration cell.
